The value of research has always rested on a quiet assumption: that the people answering are real, that each of them is one person, and that they are who your study needs them to be. That assumption is getting expensive to hold. This paper is about the premium that real human data now commands, why the market has not priced it in yet, and the one change that recovers most of it.

## The premium, defined

Every dataset carries a mix. Some share of it is genuine, unique respondents, and some share is duplicates, bots, professional survey-takers, and survey farms. The genuine share is what your decisions actually rest on. Call the gap between the two the human data premium: the extra value a response carries when you can prove a real, unique, live person is behind it, rather than hoping so.

For years that premium was small enough to ignore, because faking a respondent at scale was hard. It is not small anymore.

## Why the premium is rising

The most useful single source we point people to is a research brief from NORC at the University of Chicago, which puts survey-fraud rates at roughly 15 to 30 percent across the industry, up to about 45 percent on some platforms, and estimates that around 40 percent of all nonprobability survey interviews in 2025 were likely fraudulent, on the order of two billion interviews. Kantar, one of the largest research firms in the world, called panel fraud "the new ad fraud," and has found researchers discarding up to 38 percent of collected data on average over quality problems. Greenbook has written about the pervasive threat of tech-enabled fraud in survey research.

<Note>
These are industry figures from NORC, Kantar, and Greenbook, not VerifyYou measurements and not a claim about any specific account. They describe how much junk is typically sitting in online data.
</Note>

The trend underneath the numbers is the part that matters. Generative AI changed the economics of faking a person. A language model produces fluent, human-sounding answers in bulk, and a bot farm spins up convincing synthetic respondents cheaply. When the cost to fake a person falls, the volume rises, and the honest share of any given dataset shrinks. The premium on the honest share rises to match. NORC found that AI-driven agents can now pass attention checks at near-perfect rates, 99.8 percent in one proof-of-concept study, which is the tell that the trap questions many teams rely on are quietly losing their grip.

## Why detection alone cannot recover it

The industry's instinct has been to catch fakes after collection: attention checks, speeding detection, open-end review, geo and device signals. These are worth running, and they are also a cat-and-mouse loop. Defenders learn the latest tell, attackers learn the new defense, and each round the synthetic responses look a little more real. You can win rounds, but you do not get to stop playing, and every round you lose is data you already paid for.

The deeper limit is one of order. Detection asks its question after the money is spent and the dataset is built. By then the waste is sunk and the contamination is mixed in with everything good. Filtering after the fact recovers some of the premium. It cannot recover the part you already paid out, and it cannot fully un-mix a poisoned set.

## The change that recovers most of it

There is a different posture available, which is to move the question to the door. Instead of asking how much of what you collected was fake, you ask whether there is a real, live, unique human here, right now, before this counts. That is the shift from reactive detection to proactive proof.

A human check is the step that makes it concrete. It confirms a real, live, unique human in a few seconds in the browser, needs no government ID, and returns a simple verified or denied. It answers two questions at once. Liveness asks whether a live person is present now, not a photo, a recording, or a deepfake. Uniqueness asks whether you have already seen and paid this person before. A duplicate fails uniqueness, a bot fails liveness, and a survey farm fails both the moment the same face appears again.

Placement is where the premium is actually recovered. Put the check right before the incentive or the payout, and real people push through because they have already done the work and want to finish, while the accounts that balk at putting a live, unique face on the line at that moment are usually the ones you were about to pay for nothing. The check becomes a filter on who collects, not a hurdle early in the flow.

## What it does not claim

A human check is additive to your quality stack, not a replacement for it. It is not KYC and does not prove anyone's name or legal identity, on purpose, because for survey work you almost never need to know who someone legally is, only that they are a real person you have not already paid. And it does not add friction for your good participants: a respondent who passes carries a portable credential across your surveys, so a genuine returning participant is recognized rather than asked to prove themselves over again.

On data, because it shapes your own liability: to recognize a returning respondent we keep a face vector, a one-way numerical representation of facial geometry, an array of numbers rather than an image. That number is not a picture and, on its own, is just a string with no name attached. What reaches your dashboard is a verdict, never a face.

## The bottom line

The market has not fully priced the human data premium yet, which is exactly why it is worth capturing now. The teams that move the question to the door stop paying for the same person twice, and for bots not at all, and they get to make decisions on data they can actually stand behind.
